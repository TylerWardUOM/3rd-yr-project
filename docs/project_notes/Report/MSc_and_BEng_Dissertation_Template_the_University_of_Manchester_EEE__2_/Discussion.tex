\subsection{Communication and Latency Constraints}
Section 6.1 shows that the dominant system constraint is communication delay, not host computation. The measured round-trip delay is much larger than the nominal 1~ms haptic update period, so loop delay rather than processing speed sets the practical limit on achievable stiffness.

This is consistent with delay-limited haptic behaviour: added phase lag reduces stability margins and increases sensitivity to oscillation. The burst-like host packet timing and fixed-latency RTT profile also suggest that transport and scheduling overhead in the communication stack are likely major contributors.

The implication is that communication architecture should be treated as a first-order design variable in low-cost haptic systems, alongside control and mechanics.

\subsection{Stability of the Haptic Interaction}
Despite delay and bursty packet timing, stable interaction was achieved in Section 6 through architectural decoupling and conservative control shaping.

Snapshot-based state exchange prevented the control loop from acting on stale queued data, while separate communication, rendering, and simulation threads reduced timing interference across subsystems. At the actuator level, damping and torque rate limiting suppressed sharp force transients that previously drove oscillation.

Together, these choices favoured robust behaviour over aggressive stiffness, enabling repeatable and controllable contact interaction under non-ideal timing conditions.

\subsection{Hardware Limitations}
Section 6.4 indicates that hardware limitations were a second major constraint after communication delay.

First, torque control remained qualitative because the final motor driver path did not include current sensing, so commanded torque could not be mapped to calibrated physical torque. Second, the transmission introduced high-load non-idealities (including belt slip), reducing force consistency near the upper operating range. Third, final validation used partial actuation after loss of one actuator, reducing full 2-DOF force-feedback capability during physical testing.

These factors explain the observed compliant contact feel and finite maximum practical stiffness.

\subsection{Design Trade-Offs}
The final design prioritised stability and safety over maximum rigidity. Damping and rate limiting improved controllability and prevented unstable contact events, but they also softened sharp contact transients and reduced apparent stiffness.

For this prototype, that trade-off was appropriate: reliable, safe interaction was achieved within low-cost hardware constraints, while clearly identifying where future improvements should focus (lower-latency communication and higher-fidelity actuation).
